17 policy platform cards — fold-and-cut. Print this page (A4 landscape), cut along the dashed borders. Each card has an OLD WAY (what they'll say) and a NEW WAY (your answer). Use alongside the Pocket MMT rebuttal cards on the Frontline PWA.
01Unemployment & Job Guarantee
OLD WAY
Unemployment is the natural, necessary price of keeping inflation low. The market will create jobs if we get conditions right.
NEW WAY
Unemployment is a deliberate policy choice — the government uses idle lives as its price anchor. A Job Guarantee creates a buffer stock of employed people: every willing worker gets a living-wage public job. Prices are anchored by the floor, not the dole queue.
02Dental & Mental Health Security
OLD WAY
Dental and mental health are extras — unfortunate budget pressures mean they can't be universal. Private insurance should fill the gap.
NEW WAY
Oral and mental health are inseparable from physical health. A tooth infection or untreated depression costs the economy far more than the clinic. The currency issuer can fund any clinic it chooses — the real constraints are trained workers, equipment and facilities, never the accounting.
03Housing
OLD WAY
The market will build enough homes if we cut red tape. Negative gearing rewards the investors who take the risk and increase supply.
NEW WAY
Shelter is a public utility, not an asset class. Negative gearing is a tax expenditure worth billions annually — it inflates prices and transfers wealth upward. Government can direct spending to build social housing at scale; the constraint is construction workers and materials, not money.
04Welfare & Income Support
OLD WAY
Welfare creates dependency and is unsustainable. We need targeted payments and mutual obligation to keep people job-ready.
NEW WAY
Income support is automatic fiscal stabilisation — it flows when private incomes fall and contracts as they recover. The strongest income floor is a Job Guarantee: a guaranteed living-wage job for anyone who wants one. Top-up payments cover those who can't work. Compliance regimes don't create jobs.
05Education Cost
OLD WAY
Students should contribute to the cost of their education — it's an investment in themselves and they'll earn more for it.
NEW WAY
An educated population is a public good that pays dividends to the entire community — not just the graduate. The currency issuer has no revenue constraint on education spending. The real question is whether we have enough teachers, classrooms and learning materials. We do.
06HECS & Student Debt
OLD WAY
HECS is manageable — you only repay when you earn above the threshold. It's a reasonable cost-sharing arrangement.
NEW WAY
Student debt is a lifetime tax on curiosity, risk-taking and public-sector careers. The government that created the dollars also created the debt — it can cancel both with a ledger entry. The real cost of education is training teachers and building schools, not accounting entries. Debt cancellation costs nothing in real terms.
07Finding Work & Employment Services
OLD WAY
Job seekers need to try harder and meet obligations. Privatised job agencies provide competition and efficiency in placement.
NEW WAY
In a demand-constrained economy there are always more seekers than vacancies — compliance regimes don't create jobs, they humiliate people for a structural failure. A restored Commonwealth Employment Service matches skills to real work and pairs with the Job Guarantee so there is always a vacancy to offer.
08Banking & Public Finance
OLD WAY
Private banks serve the market efficiently. A public bank would be inefficient, politically captured and distort credit allocation.
NEW WAY
Private banks create money through lending and direct it wherever is most profitable — often existing asset prices, not new productive capacity. A public bank directs credit toward housing, climate infrastructure and regional development where private banks won't go. It existed before; it can exist again.
09The Economy & Fiscal Policy
OLD WAY
A balanced budget is responsible government. Deficits crowd out private investment and leave debt for future generations.
NEW WAY
The government's financial deficit is the non-government sector's surplus — accounting arithmetic. A currency issuer that forces surpluses during a slowdown withdraws spending power from households. The real constraint is inflation, not the ledger. Run the deficit the economy needs to keep people employed.
10Renewables & Public Energy
OLD WAY
Renewable energy is too expensive and unreliable without market price signals. Subsidies distort investment decisions.
NEW WAY
The sun and wind are free — the cost is turbines, cables and engineers. A currency-issuing government can fund a grid transformation at will. Public ownership of generation and transmission means the public captures the free fuel dividend instead of private shareholders.
11Just Transition
OLD WAY
We can't destroy coal communities for climate ideology. The jobs will vanish and regional towns will be abandoned.
NEW WAY
The government can guarantee every coal and gas worker a new public-sector job before the pit closes — at the same wage, in the same town where possible. A just transition is the Job Guarantee applied sector by sector, funded by the currency issuer at no accounting limit.
12Fast Rail
OLD WAY
Fast rail costs hundreds of billions — it's a fantasy we simply cannot afford in the current fiscal environment.
NEW WAY
Australia has had the engineers, the steel, the flat corridors and the right-of-way for decades. The currency issuer spends infrastructure into existence; the constraint is scheduling the construction workforce — not finding the money. Every year of delay is a deliberate policy choice, not a financial necessity.
13Regional Transport
OLD WAY
Regional bus and rail lines lose money — the government can't subsidise unviable services indefinitely. Let the market decide.
NEW WAY
Public transport is social infrastructure, not a profit centre. The real cost of no service is isolation, structural unemployment and health outcomes — all of which cost the public sector more in the long run. Regional communities like ours deserve the same connectivity as inner-city suburbs.
14Power Bills & Energy Guarantee
OLD WAY
High power bills are a market problem — the solution is more competition, not government intervention in pricing.
NEW WAY
Electricity is a natural monopoly — "competition" in transmission is a fiction. Public ownership gives government direct control of the price. An energy price guarantee — a regulated maximum — protects households, anchors inflation expectations and ends energy poverty in one move.
15Food Security
OLD WAY
Imported food is cheaper — global markets handle supply more efficiently than government programs ever could.
NEW WAY
Supply chains are fragile — pandemics and trade wars prove it instantly. Domestic food production capacity is a sovereign strategic asset. Government can support small-farm production, regional food processing and grain reserves without any accounting constraint; the real limits are land, water and agronomists.
16Indigenous Justice & Self-Determination
OLD WAY
Closing the Gap needs better-targeted programs with community accountability. We can't afford open-ended commitments.
NEW WAY
Self-determination means communities control their own resources, institutions and futures. The constraints on mob-controlled enterprises, community legal services and land management are political will and trained people — not money. Treaty is the framework that makes everything else legitimate. The currency issuer can fund it.
17Farming & Agricultural Transition
OLD WAY
Family farms must compete in global commodity markets without subsidies. That's what free trade requires and it drives efficiency.
NEW WAY
Food sovereignty is a strategic asset — farmers are the frontline of national resilience. Government can fund climate-adapted farming research, water infrastructure, soil health programs and ecological transition without any revenue limit. The constraints are agronomists, water engineers and seed banks — not accounting entries. Cowra knows this.
